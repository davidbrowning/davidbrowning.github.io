KENNETH H BROWNING

AUTOBOGRAPHY

 

            I was born February 27, 1939 in Sugar, Idaho.  My father’s name is Charles Henry Browning, and my mother’s name is Emma Margaret Hill Browning.

 

            The place I remember as home as a child was Victor, Idaho.  Victor is a small farming community located in the Teton basin, west of the Grand Teton Peaks and just over that mountain from Jackson Hole, Wyoming.   It had a total population of 150.  Here is where I remember most of my childhood days for we lived there until I was twelve years old.  Dad was a butcher, and owned his own slaughter house just west of town.  I can remember spending many days playing there at the slaughter house and helping dad with his work.

            Both my father and mother had been previously married.  So on my father’s side, I have two brothers, Cleo and Rich, and two sisters, Geneva and Florence.  On my mother’s side, there was my sister, Margaret, and a brother, Floyd Robert “Bob”.  There were three of us boys that were born to Mom and Dad.  The oldest was LeRoy.  I was in the middle, then my youngest brother, Irven.  Roy and I were constantly fighting and arguing as we grew up.  Our mother said that I was a good natured child and could play for hours with two cans and some dirt.  I would sit and pour the one can of dirt into the other and keep myself amused for a long time until Roy would come and kick them over or take them away.  Once when we were teenagers, Mom had had her fill of our fighting.  She told us that the next time she caught us fighting she would take us outside and roll up our pant legs.  We would fight and each time we would stop she would swat our bare legs with a willow.  Soon Roy had had enough so he took off running.  Mom chased him, caught him, and swatted him all the way home.  Mom was so exhausted by the time they got home that she went into the house and forgot about me.  This was one time that Roy got the worst of it.  Irven was five years younger than me, and he was my tag-a-long.  He always wanted to go with me, and I usually took him with me until I grew older and had friends I wanted to be with.  All of dad’s children except for Rich, who was the youngest, were married before I was old enough to remember, and they seemed more like aunts and uncles to me than brothers and sisters.  Consequently, I had lots of nieces and nephews older than myself.  On one occasion when I was about 11, my seventeen year old nephew, who was almost 6 feet tall, said to his friends “Meet my Uncle Ken.”  Rich lived with us a few years that I can remember, but the main thing I can remember of Rich before he got married was when he came home from the service he brought two friends with him.  It seemed so funny because Rich is only about 5 foot 6 inches tall, and both of his friends were so tal that they had to actually duck as they went through the doorways in our home.  As far as Margaret and Bob, I always think of them just as full brothers and sisters because I grew up around them.

 

            During my boyhood, I would go to school in our one room schoolhouse, help dad in his slaughter house, ski, and have dog sled races with my dog, Pete.  Pete was the fastest dog in Victor.  When I was about 5, I had my tonsils removed and while recovering I ate a soda cracker and started hemorrhaging.  My mother quickly put me in the car and took off for the nearest hospital.  About half way there, I threw up and it was all blood.  Mom was panicked and was close to the Indian Reservation so she drove there as she knew I would probably not make it to the hospital.  She found a doctor, but he didn’t want to touch me since I wasn’t native American and didn’t live on the reservation.  Finally, when he saw me coughing up blood, he quickly took care of me and saved my life.  Thanks mainly goes to Heavenly Father for blessing us that we could be there in time.           

            All of us kids were encouraged to attend church and were there taught the principles of the gospel.  Dad had been ordained a High Priest, mainly because of his age. Dad wasn’t the most active high priest in the ward and in his later years in Sunset would occasionally attend Priesthood meeting, if he was asked to attend.  I guess most of my encouragement and training in the church was given me by my mother and grandmother, especially my grandmother as mom worked, but grandma was home with us.

            (I need to make an insertion here, not to discredit my mother in any way, as she had many experiences in life to challenge most.  I believe that you can get a better idea of her life by reading her personal history.)

 

This a letter I wrote Sunday, August 01, 2010

 

Dear Granddaughter Ashley,

            Grandpa had hoped that we might drive home to be with you on this special day in your life.  I am sorry that my health is stopping Grandma and me from doing that.  When we found out that I was going to have this operation and we were told that I could not work for a couple of weeks, we thought that it might work out so that we could come home for this special occasion but I guess Grandpa is getting to old for my body to heal that fast.  I guess it is like waking up on a school day being too sick to go to school but also to sick to get up and play. 

            May I still tell you some of my feeling as to what this special day means to me?  Of course this special day that I am writing about is the day you are BAPTIZED.  Although it was over 63 years ago that I was baptized, I still remember that day.  I am sorry to say that it was not because of my mother or father’s testimony, teachings or encouragement that I was baptized but because of the love of my primary teacher.  When she realized that my parent’s faith was not strong enough to teach me the importance of baptism into the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter Day Saints, she continued to teach me each week in our primary class and then, several months after my 8th birthday, she arranged for me to have an interview with my Bishop.  She then picked me up on the appointed day and took me to the Stake Baptismal Day which was held at our Stake Center over 10 miles away from where I lived, in the next community.  There is not much that I remember about the meeting except that I didn’t know anyone that was there, except my primary teacher.  I do not remember the talks or the music and I did not even know the one that baptized me.  I do remember that after being baptized that I felt good and I also remembered that my teacher loved me.  The next day was Fast Sunday and my Uncle Albert, who was also serving in the bishopric, confirmed me a member of The Church of Jesus Christ of Ladder Day Saints.  I do not remember if any of my family were at the meeting or not that day.

            When looking at how much I remembered about my baptismal day, some people may say, “Why do we baptize a child so young?  Why not wait until they are older, maybe 16 or 18 or even 21 when they are an adult and can decide for themselves?”  Yes we have many wonderful and faithful members of the Church that were not baptized until they were much older.  I believe that for some reason; only known to Our Heavenly Father, some of us were blessed with the privilege of coming to this earth with special parents and circumstances wherein we are blessed to have the privilege of being baptized as early in our life as 8 years of age.  We know from our scriptures that, Our Heavenly Father has stopped Satan from having power over the hearts of little children until 8 years of age.  I believe that because of the Great Love Heavenly Father has for each of his children, as soon as circumstances are right, that He wants us to have a very special helper, a personal guide and a comforter, to be with us as we go through the rest of our life.  Most of the time, we refer to this helper as the Holy Ghost.  Ashley, for you that time is now. 

            May I testify to you, as one of many who loves you very much, that I know the Holy Ghost.  He has helped me make important decisions in my life.  He has given me answers to my prayers, often instantly.  He has comforted me when I was afraid and needed help when there was no one else that could help me.  The greatest experience to me was when I needed help and asked to know if the Book of Mormon was true and if Joseph Smith was a Prophet of God.  That answer came to me as I left my bed room, after I had completed reading the Book of Mormon and earnestly asked in prayer, as clear and certain as if someone had spoken to me in a loud voice.  I bear testimony that I know that the Book of Mormon is true and that Joseph Smith was called of God to restore the Church of Jesus Christ.   He remained true to that calling and sealed his testimony by giving his life rather than to deny his testimony.  By the Spirit of the Holy Ghost you or anyone can also have that testimony.

Love Grandpa Browning

 

While in Victor I graduated from Primary and was ordained into the Aaronic Priesthood as a Deacon.  The only grandparent I remember is my grandmother Hill.  Grandpa Hill died before I could remember him.  My father’s mother died when he was still a teenager, and his father was murdered when a bandit held up his grocery store.  Grandma Hill came to live with us a couple of years after grandpa died, and lived with us until her death in January 1959.  I’ll always be thankful to her for the guidance she gave to all of us.

 

            As I said, Dad wasn’t the most active person in the ward and during the summers I remember many choice weekends in the hills that were so near to us.  I guess it was from dad that I gained my great love for camping, but my love for fishing came from my brother, Bob.  One thing dad did teach us though was the value of work and being honest to your fellowman.  I don’t think dad ever made an unfair deal or cheated anyone in his life, in fact, he expected the same from almost everyone he met and because of this it made him feel bad to remind people that they still hadn’t paid their bills.  Because of this, he finally lost his business in 1951.  After losing his business, he went to work for a meat packing company in Butte, Montana.  He would drive home every weekend to be with the family, and then have to leave Sunday afternoon in order to be back Monday morning.  On one of those trips home, the steering column on the pickup broke causing him to wreck the truck and almost lose his life.  (I’ll tell about this experience later**) After getting out of the hospital and getting his strength back, it left him without any transportation, and therefore, no way to go back to his job.  Finally in the summer of 1952, dad came to Utah looking for work and got a job with Hill Air Force Base as a painter.  Later that summer the rest of the family moved to Utah.  We brought Pete with us, but we couldn’t keep him in the city as he would stop traffic everywhere as he was used to going where ever he pleased and we couldn’t seem to keep him tied up.  While I was away at school, dad sold Pete.  However, Pete found his way home again.  When the man came to get him, dad had me put Pete in the truck.  That was one of the hardest things I have ever had to do.  Once Pete was in the truck he never looked back and we never saw him again.

 

            Maybe I could just give a couple of choice memories that took place in Victor before going on into our move to Ogden.  Once experience I shall always remember is when dad gave me a small wiener pig.  It was probably one of the few trained pigs, but I had him trained so that he would come when I called him.  He would eat out of my hand.  The only problem was that he grew so big that he could not raise himself up after lying down so every time he needed to go down to the creek to drink water, dad had to raise him up with a pole.  Finally dad felt that he had to get rid of him because of his size so one day when we were at school, dad went ahead and butchered him.  He had to sell all of the meat because no one in the family would eat it.  While my pig was growing up we used to ride him all over.

            Another one of my choice experiences as a young boy was my dog, Pete.  Pete was a cross between an English stag hound and a greyhound.  When full grown he weighed between 95 and 100 pounds.  He stood about three to three and one half feet tall, and could put his paws on the top of a full grown man’s shoulders.  There in Victor we had hard winters which lasted at least six to seven months with the snow always covering the fences.  I trained Pete as a sled dog, and had made a regular dog sleigh for him to pull.  Because of his breeding he was a fast running dog, and in racing him I was always able to win all of the other fellows in town.  I can remember one fall as we went up onto the hills to get our winters supply of firewood. (yes, we used to have firewood that we chopped every night and had to carry in coal for the stove.)  We took Pete with us in the back of the truck, but when we filled it with logs it made him nervous to ride on it so he jumped off.  My mother was driving so dad said to see how fast he could run.  We clocked him for a little over thirteen miles at 55 miles an hour.  Within the next mile he slowed down and stopped.

 

            I met Mae Porter when our family moved to Sunset four or five years later.  I didn’t date her for a couple of years as my friends were dating girls from Roy, so they introduced me to girls that I dated.  After graduating from Davis High School I joined the Air Force.  While in Basic Training, I had a heat stroke and ended up in the hospital with a fever of 106 degrees.  They packed me in ice and then poured rubbing alcohol over me.  When I was well enough, they sent me home with a medical discharge.  I decided to go to college and thought I would go into airplane mechanics.  They had a course at the college in Pocatello so I went to stay with my Uncle Irven and Aunt Bernice and their son, Leslie.  I got a job at a service station, but found that the course I had hoped to enroll in, wasn’t open that semester so I came home.  Shortly after arriving home, my best friend, Hendrick Brandenburg, wanted me to get a date and double with him to the Stake Sweetheart Ball.  Since it was a last minute late, I had trouble getting someone.  I had called 7 girls and all were busy, so I was about to give up when Hendrick said “What about asking Mae Porter?”  Mae said yes, but we went alone to the dance as Hendrick’s girlfriend got sick and so they couldn’t go.  I dated Mae for eight months when I asked her to marry me and gave her an engagement ring.  We were engaged for eight months and were married after she graduated from high school on the 19th of June 1959.

 

            I have always been restless regarding my life’s occupation.  Many times I have startled Mae when I would come home from work and say: “Lets move to Washington, I hear they need programmers up there, or lets go look at farms.”  I always felt I should have been born 100 years ago.  I used to think I was born in the wrong generation for I like hard work and think I would forgo the luxuries and comforts of this day to the slow and simple life of the farmer 100 years ago.

 

            I have always had a strong testimony of the gospel and been active in church work.  In fact, in one ward we moved into, we had lived there for over four months and still hadn’t been asked to take a position.  We were about to change wards as I couldn’t stand to be idle in the church.  I have always held a position since I was 14 years old.    The next week I was sustained as Aaronic Priesthood Secretary.  Some of the positions I have held are:  Sunday School teacher, M.I.A. President, Elders Quorum President and counselor, Aaronic Priesthood secretary;, 1st counselor in the Bishopric twice, and executive secretary.
