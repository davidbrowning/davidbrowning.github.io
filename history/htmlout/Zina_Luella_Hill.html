Zina Luella Crandell Hill

Thoughts and Remembrances

By Kenneth H Browning

 

            Zina is my Grandmother on my mother’s side.  She was married to William M. Hill.  Grandpa died of a heart attack when I was three years old so I don’t remember him.  I remember of going to Sugar City where they lived.  Their home was white and had a screened porch.  I believe that after Grandpa’s death Grandma stayed with Aunt Vida for a short time.  The apartment on the West of our home became vacant and I think it was then that Aunt Welma and Grandma moved in there.  Uncle Lee was in the military because of World War II.  At the end of the war, Uncle Lee and Aunt Welma returned to Teton.  I remember returning to Grandma and Grandpa’s home in Sugar City and the grass had grown so tall that they had to get a neighbor to come in with a hay mower to cut it before a regular lawn mower could be used.  Grandma came to live with us from that time until her death on December 31, 1958.

 

            I remember that Bob would sleep in the attic above Grandma’s apartment and I believe that after Welma left Margaret would sleep with Grandma.  After Margaret got married and Bob went into the service, Mrs. East who’s husband died moved back to Victor.  She was the owner of the house and the Mother-In-Law to Harold Homes, who lived to the house south of us.  Mrs. East moved into the apartment, which Grandma had been living in, and Grandma moved in with us.  Occasionally Grandma would go for a visit to her other children, but home was with us.  I had a great love and respect for my Grandmother and thought there was no finer woman on earth.  Grandma was always a very stately person and her appearance was always very neat and well groomed. At home she would wear a house dress but even that was always clean and neat.  When she went anywhere outside the house she always dressed her best.  She helped me with Church assignments and school homework, giving me lots of encouragement.  Often in the evening she would have me brush her hair and often I would trim the corns and bunions from her feet and toes.  She truly became a second mother to me.  Mother worked most of the time outside of the home, but Grandma was there to help us children.  In 1952 dad got a job with Hill Air Force so our family to move to Utah, Grandma moved there with us.  In November 1954, on Thanksgiving night Uncle Myron and Aunt Lila, their daughter Joyce and her husband and Uncle Grant and Aunt Margie were all killed in an automobile accident.  Uncle Myron had been in the Veterans Hospital in Salt Lake for an eye operation to restore the sight in his eye.  He was released that morning to return home.  Uncle Grant and the others had came down to take him home.  They stopped and had Thanksgiving dinner with us in Ogden.  As they left our place Uncle Myron made the comment that if he never ate another meal, he was sure full then.  On the way home, a drunk ran into their car and killed all six of them.  Grandma received some insurance money from that accident.  She used some of that money to make the down payment on a home in Sunset for Mom, Dad and our family on the condition that she could live there until her death.

 

            Grandma seemed to me to have had her share of trials.  During the flue academic of 1912 three of her children died with the flue, all with in 48 hours.  Grandma went into a great depression and even some of the neighbors felt that she had lost her mind and needed to be hospitalized.  She said that one day she looked out the window and there stood ------- holding the baby in her arms and ------ by the hand with the most peaceful look and she know that her children were ok.  She was able to recover with that and continued with life.

 

            Once Grandma told me of a spiritual experience she had that I have always remembered and wondered about it.  I would like to preface this experience by telling a little about her earlier marriage to Grandpa Hill.  William M. Hill was a lumber jack for many of their early years of marriage and while raising their family.  As a result of this occupation the family lived in the hills and timber land during much of the family’s early years.  The boys grew up without the Church activity to influence their lives.  As they chose girls to marry, they were not members of the Church.  All the boys served in the military during World War II.  Myron, Irven and Grant served in the navy and Floyd served in the Marines.  When their father died, none of the boys were active in the Church.  My mother, Aunt Vida and Aunt Wilma were active.  There were three other children born to the family and I will say more about that later.  Because the family lived in the timbering areas, none of the family attended Church.  From all I was told Grandpa was a honest, hard working individual and instilled this in all his children.  I have been told that Grandpa could stack a deck of cards, while the sharpest of card players watched him, and he could deal out any hand he wanted to any player.  Often being asked to demonstrate this ability to others.  I was always told that even with this ability he was never accused of cheating at anything.  On one occasion when Bob was a young boy, Grandpa and Bob would often play checkers.  Grandpa would always win.  On one occasion Grandpa had to be excused for a minute during a game.  While he was gone Bob switched the position of one of the pieces.  When Grandpa came back and looked at the board, he said, “You have moved a piece and that is cheating.  I don’t play games with cheaters.”  It took Bob several months of asking forgiveness and begging before Grandpa would forgive him and play a game with him again but with a warning that he never cheat again.  When Grandpa grew older and moved into town, he was deaf.  Without the aid of speaker systems and hearing aids, like we have today, he couldn’t hear the speakers and the program, so he stayed home.  Looking at the Church records you would say that William Hill was inactive at his death.  Grandma told me that one night after Grandpa’s death, he appeared standing at the foot of her bed.  She set up in bed and he said to her, “Zina, unless you do more to getting our boys active in the church than what you are doing, you will not be able to come to where I am.”  That is all that Grandma said happened, but she did work more with her family and before she died all the children except Uncle Floyd were active in the Church and had been to the Temple.

            When Grandma was a young girl, she and her sister Irene were out picking huckleberries just a short distance from the house.  Having picked their buckets full and evening setting in, they started for home.  As they were walking they heard a baby cry.  Zina had been told by her parents that the cry of a mountain lion sounded like a baby’s cry.  She told Irene to hurry for home and not to stop.  As they hurried the sound seem to be just behind the bushes and to the side of them.  The two told their parents about the experience and the next morning their father took a rifle and went out to the area that the girls had came.  He found prints of a large cougar that must have followed them all the way to within a few yards of the house.

            The following experience was told to me, but I am not sure if I have the details correct or not.  I don’t know if any one else heard or can remember the experience or not.  If I can I will try to get a copy of this to Aunt Wilma and she might be able to remember it.

 

            Hyrum Oscar Crandell is my Great Grandfather on my mother’s side.  He was married to Margaret Emma Guyman, my Great Grandmother and to Margaret’s younger sister in a plural marriage.  During the time that the United State Government was outlawing the practice of plural marriage and arresting any that were in this agreement of plural marriage.  For the safety of the family, Hyrum decided to take his family to Mexico.  This was being done by many families to escape the laws being forced on them.  The family loaded their belongings into wagon’s and with teams started the journey from Idaho to Mexico.  I guess there was many trials with such a journey but the one that I remember of being told about occurred as the family was in Southern Utah.  They found out that the sheriffs posse was after them and would probably catch up to them the next day.  They broke camp and had Hyrum’s oldest son, Hyrum Crandell “Jr.” stay behind.  When the posse came into the Sharif asked for Hyrum Crandell.  Hyrum Jr. said I am Hyrum Crandell.  The sharif them took him under arrest.  The Sharif them put Hyrum Jr. on his horse and took him back to the closest constable.  When questioned by the constable, Hyrum Crandell Jr. was able to testify that not only was he not guilty of practicing polygamy but that he wasn’t even married.  It was his father that they were looking for.  The law had to release Hyrum and by that time the rest of the family had traveled a safe distance away.  When he family arrived in Mexico Hyrum built a nice home and got a farm started.  After getting things set for the youngest wife he took his first wife back to Idaho and never saw his second family again.  One of the daughters kept a day by day journal of this experience but when it was all over the family decided that some one might get a hold of the journal and cause trouble so the journal was burned.
