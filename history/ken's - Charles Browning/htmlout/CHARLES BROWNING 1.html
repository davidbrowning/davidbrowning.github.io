CHARLES BROWNING – SLAUGHTER HOUSE

 

            My Father, Charles Henry Browning, was a butcher by trade; at least from the time I was old enough to remember him.  Although he had owned a dry farm and managed a grain elevator before I was born.  I think those occupations are recorded in his history as recorded by Betsy Browning.  I, therefore, will not at this time re-tell those experiences.

            My first remembrance was that Dad ran a meat retail department in Larsen’s General Mercantile Store on Main Street in Victor.  I would often walk to the store and enjoy spending time with my Dad.  He had a refrigeration room that he always kept various dressed out animals hanging there.  He almost always had a large wooden pickle barrel in there.  I remember that I would ask Dad for a nickel to buy candy and when he said that he didn’t have a nickel, I would reply with, “then can I have a pickle?”  That always got a chuckle and a “yes” reply. 

            Dad had to buy his meat from other slaughterhouses or kill the animal in the field.  He also built a small shed out back of the store and did some killing there.  I think that new USDA laws finally forbid him from using that shed and frowned on him killing in the field.  Finally Dad bought a five acre lot just outside the North West City Limits of Victor.  There he built his own slaughterhouse for the purpose of killing his own animals to supply meat for the Butcher shop.  I remember watching the construction of the building.  The cement was mixed in a small electric cement mixer, poured into wheel barrels, which were pushed by hand, to hand built forms over 2 X 12 planking.  The original building was about 24’ X 40’ and was divided into four rooms.  The N.W. room was the killing room, the N.E. room was the cutting and wrapping room, the S.W. room was the cooling room and the S.E. room was the refrigeration room.  When Dad first started killing there he did all his cutting by hand.  My brother Rich was in the army and in one of Mom’s letters to him she told him that Dad was working hard and often came home very tired, with sore aching muscles after cutting an animal by hand.  Dad was still running the shop in the store and did the butchering after hours or would have Mom run the shop while he worked at the slaughterhouse.  On receiving this letter, Rich found a Sears and Roebuck catalog and with some of the money he had saved, from his military pay, he ordered an electric band saw for Dad.  It wasn’t very long after Dad started operation in the slaughterhouse that the decision was made to quit the shop in the grocery store and to go full time with the slaughterhouse.  Many people in the valley wanted Dad to do the killing of their animals instead of taking them to the Driggs slaughterhouse.  Also, Dad found a lot of requests for him to furnish meat to the restaurants and Dude Ranches in Jackson Hole, Wyoming.

            The following summer Dad added a fifth room onto the North end of the slaughterhouse.  He also built a large chicken coop and a pig barn further north of the Slaughter House.  In the chicken coop Dad kept approximately 300 laying hens.  This kept him with chickens to kill for meat and eggs to sell. 

 

            Here are a couple of incidences which happened in connection to the slaughterhouse.  I’ll add others at a later date.   

 

            On the South West corner of the block where we lived was a hotel.  About the same time Dad was getting his slaughterhouse going, a family by the name of Burnside became the manager of that hotel.  Mr. Burnside also had a hobby of raising and running sled dogs.  At first he kenneled them just behind our lot.  That soon caused a problem with many citizens of the community as the dogs were always barking day and night.  To add to those problems, the dogs often broke out of their kennel and caused a nuisance in town.  He was finally told that he had to kennel his dogs outside the city limits.

            Mr. Burnsides set up his Dog Kennels, just across the road from Dad's slaughter house.  The dogs would still get loose and almost every time they did they would go for Dad's chickens.  At first it was just a few chickens, but one night four or five dogs got out killing almost 200 chickens.  I remember helping with the cleaning of all those chickens.  Dad saved what we could.  Dad then took the chickens up to Mr. Burnsides and told him he had just purchased them.  Mr. Burnsides had to buy a freezer to keep them in.  It was just a few days later that Dad and I went to the Slaughter House, and as we got out of the pickup, we could hear chickens squawking.  Dad jumped the fence and ran for the coop door.  Just as he arrived, a dog came out the chicken run.  Dad caught the dog by the collar, lifting it into the air, swung it around his head two or three times before bring it to the ground with all his might.  The dog was killed instantly by the crushing force.   I had just arrived at the fence to see the whole thing take place.  I was impressed with the strength Dad had.  Mr. Burnsides had to buy a few more chickens that day.  From then on he also tried to keep his kennels in better repair.

 

            About a half mile south of Dad’s slaughterhouse lived a man named Ceiler Westover and his sister.  The sister was crazy and Ceiler was considered a moron or had a very low IQ.  Dad often had Ceiler do some work around the slaughterhouse and would pay him a little.   Often the pay would be with some meat to help them out.  Dad had tracks on the ceiling of the slaughterhouse on which he would hang meat and could easily move the meat to and from the killing room, the cooling room, the refrigeration room or to the a loading area.  One day, Dad was cutting meat and asked Ceiler to bring a half of beef from the cooling room.  As Ceiler was pushing it along the track, Dad looked up to see that Ceiler had not switched the track properly and the switch was open.  Dad hollered, but Ceiler was coming too fast to stop.   Dad ran and as the roller came off the track, Dad caught the beef in his arms.  The roller came down and hit Dad's head causing a large cut.   I remember watching the blood running down his neck and him carrying the half beef to the cutting table and putting it down with out it touching the floor.  That was another impressive moment of Dad's strength.

 

            When Dad had put the fence around the slaughter house property he had put the entrance gate coming to the South West corner of the building.  As a result, every time he wanted to load out meat, he had to open the gates to drive in, and then close the gates so no animals could get out.  He decided to change the gates to the East side.  The gates consisted of two gates, each about 10 feet long, swung off heavy posts.  The post stood about 10 feet high and had two cross logs at the top going from one post to the other.  This made quite a heavy frame, but Dad thought he could move the frame without disassembling it.  The upright post had also been set in concrete and about 3 to 4 feet deep.  We therefore dug around the concrete and planned to break the cement off with a sludge hammer.  Dad and I were just finishing digging around the second post when the whole frame began to move.  Dad hollered for us to move as he thought the frame might be tipping over, but to our amazement Ceiler had squatted down and put his arms around the first post and was lifting the post, and cement out of the hole.  Dad and I both watched in amazement as to Ceiler’s strength.
